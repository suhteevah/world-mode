## Summary

### Key Lessons
- Gradual conversion strategy: keep old systems running while new modules come online, then decommission old ones piece by piece
- Two new iron modules at 723 plates/second each = 1,446 plates/second total, equal to roughly 4 old-style smelting setups
- Steel production must be maintained during iron conversion -- dedicate lower ore patches to steel while upper patches go to new iron modules
- Converting old smelting to steel production reuses existing infrastructure without total rebuild
- Wagon production can silently bottleneck new module deployment -- ensure assemblers have fast enough inserter feeding

### Design Principles
- Convert infrastructure gradually: new online first, then old offline, never both down simultaneously
- Geographic resource planning: partition ore patches by product type (iron vs steel) based on location relative to main bus
- When deconstructing old builds, remove inner components first while keeping rail connections alive until replacement is confirmed working
- T-intersections at module boundaries allow future expansion without redesigning the rail layout
- Monitor iron supply warnings as an expected temporary condition during transition -- but do not ignore them for too long

### Ratios & Numbers
- 2 new modules: 723 plates/second each = 1,446 total (twice output of old system combined)
- Steel stockpile: 9,000-35,000 range = at risk of running out during transition
- 21 trains allocated to iron network at this stage
- 8 trains minimum needed per new smelting module deployment
- Old smelting patches with <35,000 ore remaining are candidates for decommission rather than conversion

### Mistakes to Avoid
- Do not decommission all old iron smelting at once -- gradual conversion prevents resource crashes
- Steel production gaps are easy to overlook while focused on iron conversion; monitor steel stockpiles actively
- Wagon production shortfalls silently prevent new train deployment -- check assembler throughput
- Do not assume removing old rail connections is harmless until the replacement system has proven stable under load
