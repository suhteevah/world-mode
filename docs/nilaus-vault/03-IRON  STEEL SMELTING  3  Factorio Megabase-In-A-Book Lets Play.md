## Summary
### Key Lessons
- Iron replacement is more complex than copper because it also requires a steel smelting design
- The iron smelter blueprint is essentially the same as copper - just rename the stations (the power of modular design)
- Steel smelting requires a different blueprint because it takes iron plates as input (not ore) and has longer crafting times
- Fueling remains a critical unsolved problem at this point - no automatic refueling train yet
- Construction robots are painfully slow for building large outposts; spidertrons are needed for faster construction

### Design Principles
- Replace iron and steel together since they share the same bus space in the starter base
- Use 2 iron inbound stations and 1 steel inbound station to match consumption ratios
- Expand the train grid incrementally: add 5 new city block columns at a time for future builds
- Steel smelting uses beacon-boosted electric furnaces with the same 8-belt I/O pattern
- Stop old ore trains by setting station train limit to zero, then let the buffer drain naturally before deconstructing

### Ratios & Numbers
- Iron/steel split: 2 iron inbound locations + 1 steel inbound location for the starter base
- Steel smelting: uses iron plates as input, produces at a slower rate than iron/copper smelting
- Grid expansion: 5 new city block columns added in this episode for future build space
- Train grid: maintains consistent spacing between parallel tracks for blueprint compatibility

### Mistakes to Avoid
- Do not replace iron without also having steel ready - the base uses both from the same bus section
- Do not forget to set up fueling trains early; stations without fuel trains will eventually stall
- Construction bot charging is a major bottleneck - add extra roboports with charging pads at build sites
- Do not underestimate the rail infrastructure needed; running out of rails mid-expansion is common
