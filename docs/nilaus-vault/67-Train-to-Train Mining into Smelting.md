## Summary

### Key Lessons
- The new direct mining/smelting system uses two separate closed train loops: mining-to-smelting (internal) and smelting-to-main-network (external)
- Internal loop trains never touch the main rail network, preventing congestion interference
- Excess trains must be allocated to always have one parked at each loading station -- fundamentally different from the standard "summon when ready" approach
- The smelting module tiles vertically with 28-tile width, using snap-to-grid blueprints for repeatable placement
- Chain signals before merge points and regular signals after prevent deadlocks in the internal loop

### Design Principles
- Separate train loops: internal ore-to-smelting loop is isolated from the main network
- Train count formula: total stations (input + output + buffer) minus 1 = maximum trains (always leave one slot open for movement)
- Use chain signals at intersections so trains only enter if they can fully clear the crossing
- Place regular signals after crossings to create buffering blocks where trains can safely wait
- Blueprint width of 28 tiles with snap-to-grid enables perfect tiling of smelting modules

### Ratios & Numbers
- 10 input ore stations + 4 output plate stations + 4 buffer stations = 18 total, so 17 trains maximum
- Each smelting row: 16 furnaces at 5.025 plates/second = 80.4 plates/second
- 9 smelting rows per module = 723 plates/second total output per module
- Internal loop: ~8-12 trains circulating between mining and smelting
- Train size: 1 locomotive + cargo wagons (compact 2-segment trains for tight internal loops)

### Mistakes to Avoid
- Do not connect internal loop trains to the main rail network -- they must stay in their dedicated loop
- Extra signals in the blueprint can cause deadlocks when tiling -- verify signal placement after each tile
- Do not forget to reserve at least one empty station slot for train circulation; if all slots are filled, the system deadlocks
- Symmetry in blueprint design is important for tiling but should not override functional signal placement
